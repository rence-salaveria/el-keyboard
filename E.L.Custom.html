Keyboard Parts Breakdown

To begin our exploration, let's establish a fundamental grasp of the components comprising a keyboard.

Keyboard Case

The keyboard's outer casing, typically self-evident, encases its internal components, with options for single or multiple pieces, influencing aesthetics and functionality choices.

PCB

Introduce the PCB (Printed Circuit Board), the keyboard's brain, where you'll install switches and stabilizers. They are available in solder or hot-swap variants.

Plate

A crucial component. The plate plays a significant role by facilitating the attachment of your switches to the PCB. It ensures the switch housing securely aligns, aiding in its stability, before soldering or hot-swapping onto the PCB.

Stabilizers

They stabilize the wobble of the larger keys like the shift, enter, and space bar. These are 100% essential.

Switches

Then you will need your choice of switches. There are so many to choose from these days and we will dive into that in the article.

Keycaps

You have the opportunity to express your creativity with these options! They are available on various websites and can be categorized into a few elements: profiles, keycap sets, and artisan keycaps.

Keyboard Layout

Which keyboard size and layout do you currently use, and which one suits you best? Selecting the right keyboard setup ensures comfortable and efficient typing over extended periods. In this blog, we'll assess and compare common differences in keyboard sizes and layouts to aid your search for the perfect fit.

100% | 104-key Keyboard | Full Sized Keyboard

Full-sized keyboards, comprising numeric keypads, arrow keys, main keys, and function area, are popular due to versatility, aiding office tasks like data entry. However, they're bulky and less portable.

96% | 100-Key Keyboard | Compact Full Sized Keyboard

The 96% keyboard is primarily an adaptation of the 104-key layout, streamlining by eliminating infrequently used keys and consolidating all keys. It retains office functionality while sporting a more compact design.

80% | 87-key Keyboard | Tenkeyless Keyboard (TKL)

The 80% keyboard layout, also known as Tenkeyless (TKL), omits the numeric keypad, effectively reducing the keyboard's size by eliminating the rarely used numeric section. It's a great choice for gamers seeking a PC-style layout with enhanced desk space.

75% | 84-keys keyboard | Compact Tenkeyless Keyboard

The 75% keyboard, common in laptops, retains essential keys while compacting the layout to 84 keys, enhancing portability. If you're a laptop user, the 75% keyboard is likely your go-to choice.

65% | 68-key keyboard | Compact Keyboard

The 65% keyboard layout minimizes keys, eliminating F function keys and the numeric keypad, utilizing key combinations for these functions. It keeps essential function and directional keys, optimizing desk space for enhanced typing.

60% Keyboard | 61-key Keyboard | Mini Keyboard

The 60% keyboard is extremely compact, featuring only the number bar and alphabetical keys, improving typing efficiency by eliminating less-used keys.

Different Kinds of Keyboard Switches

Mechanical switches play a pivotal role in determining your typing experience on a keyboard, with variations in smoothness, keypress depth, resistance, and noise. These switches are broadly categorized into three types: linear, clicky, and tactile.

Linear: Smooth and consistent keystroke with quiet noise.

Tactile: A small bump on each keystroke with moderate noise.

Clicky: A small bump on each keystroke with loud click noise.

Keyboard Mounting Styles

When exploring options for a keyboard case, there are several factors to keep in mind. Firstly, consider your preferred mounting style, as there are many intriguing and enjoyable mounting styles available. I'll do my best to provide a concise summary for easy understanding.

Gasket Mount

A gasket mount uses gasket material between the plate and keyboard housing on both top and bottom sides. This gives the board a slightly more cushioned feel since the plate is not in direct contact with the other metal components of the keyboard.

Sandwich Mount

A less common mounting style that sandwiches the plate between the bottom and top case. Typically these are stiffer and produce lots of typing feedback as well as a metallic sound depending on the plate material.

Integrated Plate Mount

This is where the plate is physically part of the top case. They are very stiff. Sound can vary but it tends to lean toward very muddy sound signatures and sometimes metallic sounds.

Tray Mount

This is very common for cheaper prebuilt keyboards or cheaper cases. In their stock form, they are stiff to type on and the sound profile can widely vary on these. However, these styles of cases are the most fun to modify and can present a wonderful keyboard experience.

Top Mount

Top Mount boards are among the most popular, securing the plate to the top housing of the keyboard.

Bottom Mount

As the inverse, bottom mount boards secure the plate to the bottom housing of the keyboard.

Different Kinds of PCB

HotSwap-PCB

The PCB comes with pre-installed hot-swappable sockets for easy switch insertion, enabling effortless switch replacement without the need for soldering expertise

Soldered PCB

All switches are soldered onto it, preventing manual removal. To replace switches, you need soldering skills and appropriate tools.

Some Modification for Custom Keyboard

Mod the switches

Lubing the switches can be a good way to improve the sound quality and the feel of the linear and tactile switches. This process is a bit more time-consuming and requires more tools. Otherwise, you can purchase the pre-lubed switches so that you do not need to spend too much time doing them yourself. You can try filming switches as well.

Mod the stabilizer

To enhance typing comfort and minimize stabilizer noise, three common methods are available: stabilizer clipping, lubrication, or the Band-Aid mod. Among these, lubrication has the most significant impact on stabilizer performance. Note that these mods may not be suitable for all stabilizer types. Prior to starting a project, consider exploring online video tutorials for further guidance

Sound dampening

Sometimes the bottom case of the custom keyboard can sound hollow, and reverberate the switch ping when typing . This is most common with metal cases. Adding some soft material like sponge foam or fibers to the bottom case is an easy way to reduce the noise and hollowness inside a keyboard.
